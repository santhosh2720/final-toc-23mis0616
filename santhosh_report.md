# An Explainable Quantum-Inspired Hybrid Traffic Signal Control Framework Using QUBO, QAOA-Style Optimization, and Theory of Computation in SUMO

## Abstract
Urban traffic signal control is one of the most difficult real-time optimization problems in intelligent transportation systems because every intersection must make discrete phase decisions under continuously changing traffic demand, while also remaining coordinated with neighboring intersections. Traditional fixed-time traffic control methods are simple and stable, but they do not adapt to time-varying congestion. Reactive actuated approaches are more flexible, yet they still remain strongly local and often fail to account for corridor-level and network-level interactions. Search-based heuristics can improve performance in some cases, but they may still suffer from limited interpretability, limited coordination awareness, or poor consistency across changing traffic states. This project addresses that gap by proposing a complete, explainable, simulation-backed traffic-control framework that combines Theory of Computation concepts, graph-based traffic modeling, QUBO-based optimization, and a QAOA-style quantum-inspired search strategy inside a SUMO simulation environment.

The project is built around the idea that multi-intersection signal coordination is not merely a timing problem; it is a discrete combinational optimization problem whose complexity grows rapidly with network size. At every control interval, each signalized junction must choose one valid action from a finite set of phase alternatives. When several intersections are considered together, the total number of possible global signal assignments increases exponentially. This motivates the use of Theory of Computation concepts such as computational complexity, graph abstraction, state-space explosion, and approximate optimization. In the proposed system, the road network is represented as a graph in which intersections become computational decision nodes and roads become interaction edges through which congestion, queue spillback, and directional imbalance propagate.

The implemented framework begins by constructing a SUMO scenario from an image-derived traffic layout. SUMO then simulates vehicle movement and exposes lane-level and signal-level traffic information through TraCI. At each control step, the system extracts queue length, waiting time, occupancy, average speed, and current phase information from every controlled junction. A graph-based congestion predictor estimates short-horizon traffic pressure using adjacency-aware feature propagation. That predicted state is then combined with current measured traffic conditions to formulate a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) problem in which each possible signal action is encoded as a binary decision variable. The QUBO objective integrates local directional pressure, forecasted congestion, one-hot phase-selection constraints, and junction-to-junction coordination bonuses. A QAOA-style quantum-inspired solver then searches for a low-energy assignment. Finally, a hybrid controller refines the assignment using traffic-domain rules such as minimum green time, fairness-preserving switching, and corridor-level directional coordination before applying the final actions back into SUMO.

The significance of this project lies in the integration of these ideas into a single, practical system. The work does not claim a fully hardware-quantum traffic controller; instead, it demonstrates how quantum-inspired optimization principles can be translated into a functioning traffic-control pipeline that is measurable, explainable, and benchmarkable. The framework is evaluated against three baselines: fixed, actuated, and genetic. It also includes controller comparison tables, delay and queue bar charts, throughput and reward-score summaries, temporal behavior analysis, and a junction heatmap for spatial diagnosis. This makes the project suitable not only for implementation-oriented demonstration, but also for research-style discussion and journal presentation. The resulting framework is therefore best understood as an explainable, computationally grounded, quantum-inspired traffic-control system that connects mathematical optimization concepts with real simulation-based transportation analysis.

## 1. Introduction
Urban traffic systems are becoming increasingly difficult to manage because the scale, density, and variability of traffic demand have grown far beyond the assumptions under which classical fixed-cycle signaling was originally designed. A signal plan that works during one flow pattern may perform poorly just minutes later if directional demand changes, queue spillback occurs, or merging traffic begins to dominate one part of the network. As cities continue to experience increasing travel demand, signal control must do more than follow static timers; it must sense the traffic state, interpret the pattern, and choose actions that are locally valid and globally sensible. This need for adaptive, network-aware control is the basic motivation behind this project.

The challenge, however, is not merely practical. It is also computational. Each intersection in a signalized network has a limited set of valid actions at any control step, such as serving a north-south corridor or an east-west corridor. If only one intersection exists, the decision is simple. But as the number of controlled intersections increases, the number of possible network-wide signal assignments grows rapidly. A decision at one junction also changes conditions at neighboring junctions because vehicle platoons, queue propagation, and lane spillback create dependency across the network. Therefore, traffic signal coordination naturally becomes a graph-based combinational decision problem, not just a collection of isolated timing choices. This shift from simple control to large discrete optimization is the fundamental reason why Theory of Computation concepts are relevant to the project.

This work approaches the problem by combining three major perspectives. The first is a transportation-simulation perspective based on SUMO, which provides a realistic microscopic environment for testing controller behavior. The second is a computational perspective grounded in Theory of Computation, especially complexity growth, graph abstraction, and constraint-based decision modeling. The third is a quantum-inspired optimization perspective, where traffic phase selection is formulated as a QUBO problem and approximately solved using a QAOA-style search mechanism. The result is a hybrid framework in which graph-based prediction, optimization, and rule-based safety logic are integrated into one signal-control pipeline.

The project is also motivated by the need for explainability in academic traffic-control systems. Many intelligent traffic controllers focus only on producing lower delay values, but they do not make it easy to understand why a given phase decision was chosen. In contrast, this project includes controller comparison tables, waiting and queue charts, throughput and reward-score summaries, time-series comparisons, and a junction heatmap. These outputs make the controller easier to analyze, easier to defend in a journal or review setting, and easier to compare against conventional baselines. In short, the project is not only about improving traffic control numerically; it is also about building a system that is theoretically grounded, operationally meaningful, and clearly interpretable.

## 2. Problem Statement and Research Motivation
The central problem addressed in this project can be stated as follows: given the current traffic state of a signalized road network, determine which valid signal phase should be assigned to each controlled intersection so that vehicle delay and queue accumulation are reduced while maintaining effective throughput and stable corridor coordination. This is a dynamic decision problem because traffic demand changes over time, and it is a constrained problem because intersections cannot choose arbitrary actions; they must choose one valid phase from a finite action set. It is also a network problem because the action selected at one node changes the state of neighboring nodes.

The motivation for addressing this problem through a quantum-inspired framework arises from the mismatch between problem structure and classical naive control methods. Fixed-time control is easy to deploy but does not adapt. Actuated control responds to the present state, but often only in a local way. Genetic or heuristic search methods can improve optimization quality, but they still require a principled way to encode traffic objectives and constraints. QUBO provides a mathematically clean way to represent binary combinational decisions, and QAOA-style search provides a structured low-energy search philosophy for exploring that decision space. Therefore, the project is motivated by the idea that traffic control can benefit from optimization methods originally designed for hard combinational problems.

## 3. Research Objectives and Main Contributions
The first objective of the project is to formulate multi-intersection traffic signal selection as a mathematically structured optimization problem rather than a purely heuristic timing task. The second objective is to connect this formulation to a quantum-inspired solution approach using QUBO and QAOA-style search. The third objective is to implement the full pipeline in a SUMO environment so that claims can be validated through actual simulation metrics rather than only theoretical reasoning. The fourth objective is to make the result explainable, with interpretable comparisons and graphs rather than only raw backend outputs.

The main contributions of the project can therefore be summarized in four points. First, it provides a complete end-to-end traffic-control system that links image-inspired network generation, SUMO simulation, graph-based traffic state extraction, quantum-inspired optimization, and dashboard analytics. Second, it offers a clear Theory of Computation mapping for the traffic-control problem by showing why the task is graph-structured, constrained, and combinational. Third, it proposes a hybrid decision mechanism in which QUBO, QAOA-style search, prediction, and traffic-engineering rules are used together rather than in isolation. Fourth, it delivers a research-style evaluation and explanation layer including comparison tables, multiple performance graphs, time-series behavior, and junction heatmaps. This combination of mathematical grounding, simulation validity, and explainability is the strongest integrated contribution of the project.

## 4. Overall System Architecture and End-to-End Workflow
The complete workflow of the current project can be understood as a multi-stage computational pipeline. The first stage is scenario construction, where a traffic-layout image is transformed into a compact and simulation-friendly road network. The second stage is microscopic simulation, in which SUMO advances vehicles, lanes, and traffic lights while exposing state variables through TraCI. The third stage is observation modeling, where each intersection is converted into a structured object containing approach-level queue length, waiting time, occupancy, average speed, current phase, and phase age. The fourth stage is graph-based forecasting, where local features and neighboring features are blended to estimate short-horizon congestion. The fifth stage is binary optimization, where possible signal actions are encoded into a QUBO cost matrix. The sixth stage is QAOA-style low-energy search, where a valid assignment is chosen. The seventh stage is hybrid refinement, where traffic-domain rules ensure that the solution remains operationally realistic. The final stage is benchmarking and explanation, where performance metrics and graphs are generated for comparison and reporting.

This workflow is important because it shows that the project is not a disconnected collection of ideas. Each stage consumes the output of the previous stage and prepares the input for the next. SUMO provides the traffic state. The graph predictor enriches that state with a network-aware forecast. The QUBO builder transforms that information into a binary optimization problem. The QAOA-style solver searches the resulting energy landscape. The hybrid controller converts the optimized assignment into stable traffic-control decisions. Finally, the analytics layer interprets the result for evaluation and journal presentation. The architecture is therefore both computationally layered and conceptually coherent.

## 5. SUMO-Based Simulation Environment and Image-Derived Scenario
The current implementation uses SUMO as the simulation backbone because SUMO offers lane-level, signal-level, and vehicle-level control through TraCI while remaining suitable for reproducible academic experiments. In the implemented system, the traffic environment computes queue length, waiting time, throughput, and signal-phase behavior over a configurable episode horizon. The image-derived scenario is turned into a dedicated interchange-style network so that the project can benchmark controllers over a visually meaningful and structurally non-trivial traffic layout.

The SUMO environment in the project does not merely step the simulation; it also converts the raw network state into the computational structures needed by the higher-level algorithms. Each traffic-light-controlled node is mapped into four conceptual approaches: north, south, east, and west. The environment aggregates queue length, occupancy, average speed, and waiting time across incoming lanes for each approach, then packages these values into an intersection-level observation. These observations become the raw data on which both the graph predictor and the optimization layers operate. Therefore, the SUMO environment is more than a simulator; it is the bridge between the physical traffic network and the computational decision model.

## 6. Baseline Controllers and Benchmark Context
The benchmark in the current project compares four controllers: fixed, actuated, genetic, and hybrid. The fixed controller is the simplest baseline and represents traditional time-driven signal logic. It does not attempt to interpret the traffic state and instead serves traffic according to predetermined cyclic timing. The actuated controller is a local adaptive baseline that reacts to current directional pressure at a junction. The genetic controller is a heuristic optimization baseline that uses search-style logic to evaluate signal assignments. The hybrid controller is the proposed method and integrates prediction, QUBO, QAOA-style optimization, and rule-based refinement.

This comparison is necessary because a proposed controller has meaning only when it is evaluated relative to alternatives. A hybrid method that appears advanced in isolation may not be practically useful if it fails to outperform a simple fixed controller under the same conditions. Conversely, if it achieves lower waiting and queue values while maintaining strong throughput and reward score, then the project has a defensible research contribution. The benchmark design is therefore central to the project, not an optional add-on.

## 7. Theory of Computation Foundations
Theory of Computation is deeply relevant to this project because the traffic-control problem is inherently discrete, combinational, constrained, and graph-coupled. At every control interval, each intersection must choose exactly one phase from a finite set. If the network contains multiple intersections, then the set of all possible global actions becomes the Cartesian product of local action choices. This is exactly the type of growth that computational complexity theory warns about: as the number of decision points grows, the number of possible global assignments expands rapidly. The project uses this ToC perspective to justify why naive exhaustive search is infeasible and why structured approximate optimization is required instead.

The ToC perspective also explains why graph representation is essential. A computational problem becomes much more manageable when its entities, dependencies, and constraints are represented clearly. In this project, the traffic network is modeled as a graph in which intersections are nodes and road dependencies are edges. That representation is not only intuitive; it is computationally meaningful. It enables adjacency-based reasoning, forecast propagation, coordination terms in the QUBO, and corridor-sensitive hybrid control. Thus, ToC does not appear in the project merely as theory mentioned in the report; it directly shapes how the problem is encoded and solved.

## 8. Computational Complexity and State-Space Explosion
If a traffic network contains `N` intersections and each intersection has `P` valid actions, then the total number of possible global signal assignments is `P^N`. This exponential expression is one of the clearest reasons the project needs an optimization-driven design. Even for modest values of `N`, the search space becomes large enough that brute-force evaluation is impractical under real-time conditions. For example, if `P = 2` and `N = 10`, the global action space already contains `2^10 = 1024` assignments. If `N = 20`, the search space becomes `1,048,576` assignments. In larger systems or richer phase sets, the growth becomes more severe.

From a ToC viewpoint, this matters because it changes the entire design strategy. Rather than trying to check every possible combination, the project transforms the problem into a structured energy minimization form and uses an approximate low-energy search. This is exactly the kind of move that computational thinking encourages: first understand why the direct search is expensive, then reformulate the problem into a tractable optimization representation. This relationship between state-space explosion and approximate optimization is one of the strongest conceptual foundations of the project.

## 9. Graph Modeling, Binary Decisions, and Constraint Encoding
The project represents the traffic network as a graph `G = (V, E)` where `V` denotes the set of signalized intersections and `E` denotes the connectivity relationships between them. This graph representation is important because congestion is not independent across nodes. If one signal releases a platoon of vehicles, the next signal on the corridor may immediately experience increased queue and waiting pressure. Therefore, the current state of a junction is only partially local; it is also influenced by upstream and downstream interactions.

The binary-decision nature of the project follows from this graph view. For each node `i` and each valid action `a`, the project conceptually defines a binary variable `x(i,a)` that indicates whether action `a` is selected for node `i`. This converts traffic control into a binary decision system. However, because each node must choose exactly one valid action, the project must also encode a one-hot constraint. In mathematical terms, that means the controller must satisfy `sum_a x(i,a) = 1` for every node. This is where constraint encoding becomes crucial, and it leads directly into the QUBO formulation.

## 10. Quantum-Inspired Foundations and Optimization Mapping
The project is called quantum-inspired because it borrows the mathematical framing and optimization mindset associated with quantum combinational methods without claiming direct quantum hardware execution. The two most important quantum-inspired concepts used are QUBO and QAOA-style low-energy search. QUBO is used because traffic phase selection is naturally discrete and binary. QAOA-style optimization is used because the problem is a combinational energy-minimization task in which the controller seeks a low-cost assignment over a structured binary state space.

This mapping is meaningful because many real-world optimization problems can be reformulated into QUBO form, and traffic signal control is a strong example. Each node chooses one action, constraints can be expressed through penalties, and coordination can be represented through pairwise interaction terms. Once the problem is expressed as an energy function over binary variables, the project can apply a low-energy search strategy that mirrors the logic of QAOA even though it remains fully classical in implementation. Therefore, the project is not using the word "quantum" as decoration; it is using a mathematical workflow that genuinely aligns with quantum-inspired optimization methodology.

## 11. QUBO Formulation in the Current Project
Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization, or QUBO, is the mathematical heart of the project. In its most general form, QUBO seeks to minimize an objective of the form `x^T Q x`, where `x` is a binary vector and `Q` is a cost matrix. In the traffic-control context, each component of `x` corresponds to whether a particular action is chosen at a particular junction. The purpose of the QUBO builder in the project is to translate traffic engineering priorities into that binary energy landscape.

For each node, the current system computes directional pressure from the queue lengths of the north and south approaches and from the queue lengths of the east and west approaches. It also computes queue intensity, waiting intensity, predicted congestion, and emergency pressure. These signals are then combined into action-specific fit scores. If the action is `ns_green`, the score is increased when north-south pressure is dominant. If the action is `ew_green`, the score is increased when east-west pressure is dominant. The fitted desirability is then converted into a minimization-compatible cost by negating it. As a result, better actions receive lower diagonal costs in the QUBO matrix.

The project also encodes the one-hot validity constraint inside the QUBO. Each node must choose exactly one action, and this is imposed through a penalty term. The current configuration uses a relatively strong one-hot penalty to ensure that invalid multi-action or no-action states remain energetically unattractive. This is a crucial step because it converts a constrained binary decision problem into an unconstrained binary optimization problem that can still be solved using a matrix-based objective. Without this step, the optimizer would not have a reliable mechanism to preserve valid traffic-control assignments.

The implemented local fit logic can be summarized through the following conceptual equations. Directional pressure is first normalized:

`P_NS(i) = q_N(i) + q_S(i)`

`P_EW(i) = q_E(i) + q_W(i)`

`p_NS(i) = P_NS(i) / (P_NS(i) + P_EW(i) + epsilon)`

`p_EW(i) = P_EW(i) / (P_NS(i) + P_EW(i) + epsilon)`

Queue and waiting intensity are then combined:

`I_q(i) = min(1.5, total_queue(i) / (0.6 * max_queue))`

`I_w(i) = min(1.5, total_wait(i) / (20 * control_interval))`

`I(i) = 0.58 I_q(i) + 0.42 I_w(i)`

The local fit for the two active movement actions is estimated as:

`fit_NS(i) = 1.85 p_NS(i) - 0.75 p_EW(i) + 0.95 I(i)`

`fit_EW(i) = 1.85 p_EW(i) - 0.75 p_NS(i) + 0.95 I(i)`

Forecast congestion and emergency pressure are then added as amplifiers, and the final local QUBO diagonal cost becomes:

`c(i, a) = -fit(i, a)`

At the global level, the project conceptually minimizes:

`Minimize  Sum_i Sum_a c(i,a) x(i,a) + lambda Sum_i (Sum_a x(i,a) - 1)^2 + mu Sum_(i,j) in E Sum_a x(i,a)x(j,a)`

where `lambda` is the one-hot penalty and `mu` is the coordination bonus.

## 12. Interpreting QUBO Coefficients, Penalties, and Coordination Terms
The local QUBO cost in the current project is not arbitrary. It is based on interpretable traffic quantities. Queue intensity and waiting intensity are combined into an overall operational intensity term. Directional pressure is normalized so that the controller can compare north-south and east-west demand fairly. Forecast congestion and emergency pressure act as additional amplifiers so that the optimizer does not consider only the current moment, but also future risk and special-traffic priority. A small continuity bias is added when the currently active green phase is still productive and has not exceeded the maximum allowable duration.

The coordination bonus is another important term. If two adjacent intersections choose the same directional action and that alignment supports corridor flow, the total energy decreases. This encourages network-level consistency rather than selfish local optimization. In effect, the QUBO objective becomes a combination of local traffic fit and pairwise coordination. That balance is one of the most valuable properties of the formulation, because it allows the project to represent both intersection-level needs and corridor-level cooperation inside the same optimization structure.

## 13. Theoretical QAOA Background
The Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm, or QAOA, is one of the foundational quantum algorithms proposed for discrete combinational optimization. In theoretical quantum computing, QAOA constructs parameterized quantum states using alternating applications of a cost Hamiltonian and a mixer Hamiltonian. The goal is to tune those parameters so that the final quantum state has a high probability of collapsing to a low-cost solution of the target optimization problem. In compact mathematical form, the state is often written as `|gamma, beta> = U_M(beta_p) U_C(gamma_p) ... U_M(beta_1) U_C(gamma_1) |+>^n`, and the optimization objective is to minimize the expected energy of the cost Hamiltonian.

For this project, the value of QAOA lies in the optimization philosophy it represents. It is designed for exactly the kind of structured, binary, constrained, low-energy search problem that traffic-phase assignment becomes when encoded as a QUBO. Therefore, even though the project does not execute the full circuit model on quantum hardware, the QAOA concept still provides a useful and academically meaningful lens through which the optimization procedure can be designed and explained.

## 14. Implemented QAOA-Style Solver in the Current System
The current implementation uses a quantum-inspired classical solver rather than a gate-level quantum backend. The solver begins by grouping variables by junction, then initializes the binary assignment by selecting locally favorable actions. It computes the QUBO energy through the quadratic form `E(x) = x^T Q x`, where lower energy corresponds to a more desirable traffic configuration. It then iteratively proposes local replacements, repairs the result to preserve one-hot validity, and evaluates whether the new assignment should be accepted.

The acceptance logic uses a temperature-controlled stochastic rule similar in spirit to annealing. If a proposal lowers the energy, it is accepted. If it raises the energy, it may still be accepted with a probability determined by `exp(-Delta/T)`, where `Delta` is the energy increase and `T` is the current temperature. The temperature gradually decreases, causing the search to become less exploratory over time. This gives the solver a balance between exploration and exploitation. Although this is not exact hardware QAOA, it is a meaningful low-energy search method inspired by the same class of combinational optimization thinking. The solver therefore gives the project a legitimate quantum-inspired optimization layer while remaining deployable in a classical environment.

## 15. Graph-Based Congestion Predictor and Quantum Policy Network
The graph-based congestion predictor gives the controller a short-horizon understanding of likely traffic stress. It begins by extracting normalized node features such as queue level, waiting level, occupancy, and slowdown. Those features are then propagated across the adjacency structure of the network using weighted mixing between each node's own state and the average state of its neighbors. Nonlinear transformations are applied layer by layer, and the result is compressed into a congestion score between zero and one. This allows the hybrid controller to reason about where heavy congestion is likely to emerge, not only where it is currently visible.

The project also includes a small policy network that encodes features using sine and cosine transformations before scoring the available phase actions. The role of this component is not to replace the optimizer, but to provide a lightweight learnable refinement signal. Once policy updates accumulate enough evidence, the policy network can reinforce strong action choices when its confidence becomes sufficiently high. This gives the controller a learning-enhanced component without removing the interpretability and constraint structure provided by the QUBO and hybrid rules.

## 16. Hybrid Quantum Controller
The hybrid controller is the central decision-making component of the project. It begins by calling the graph predictor, then constructs the QUBO problem, solves it with the QAOA-style optimizer, and receives an initial assignment of actions. However, instead of applying that assignment directly, it performs several traffic-domain checks. It enforces minimum green time so that a currently active productive phase is not switched too early. It applies fairness-based switching when the opposite axis has accumulated enough pressure or waiting burden. It also avoids useless non-green holding behavior under meaningful congestion and adds corridor-sensitive coordination using neighbor votes and global directional dominance.

This hybrid design is important because it combines mathematical optimization with operational realism. A purely optimization-driven solution can produce assignments that look good numerically but are unstable or overly aggressive in live traffic. A purely rule-driven solution is easier to interpret but may miss network-wide opportunities. By combining the two, the hybrid controller gains the advantages of both. It remains explainable and traffic-aware, while still benefiting from forecast-informed global optimization. This integrated design is the strongest algorithmic contribution of the project.

The table should be interpreted in a comparative, not isolated, manner. In other words, the absolute value of a metric matters less than the relative ordering across controllers. If it also shows a lower queue mean, this suggests that the controller is not simply redistributing congestion from one junction to another, but is more effectively regulating traffic pressure across the network. If throughput remains competitive or higher, then the improvement in waiting and queue is not being achieved at the expense of traffic movement. This balance is important in research evaluation, because a controller that reduces waiting by over-prioritizing one corridor while blocking the rest of the network may appear locally good but globally poor. The comparison table therefore helps establish whether the hybrid method is genuinely balanced and network-aware.

The table is especially important in this project because it reflects the core research claim: that a quantum-inspired hybrid traffic controller can produce better signal-control outcomes than conventional fixed-time, reactive actuated, and heuristic genetic controllers. Therefore, when the hybrid row demonstrates a strong combination of low waiting, low queue, strong throughput, and high reward score, the table becomes direct evidence that the proposed controller is effective.

Another important aspect of the comparison table is that it reflects aggregated behavior across replications rather than one isolated run. ## 24. Why the Project is Quantum-Inspired Rather Than Fully Quantum
This distinction is extremely important for academic honesty and technical clarity. The current project is best described as a quantum-inspired traffic-control framework, not a fully quantum traffic-control system. The project uses QUBO formulation and QAOA-style optimization logic, both of which come directly from the intellectual toolkit of quantum optimization. However, the implemented solver currently executes on a classical machine inside a conventional software stack. The system does not run a full variational circuit on quantum hardware, does not prepare quantum superposition states across physical qubits, and does not use real quantum measurement outcomes as the final source of decision assignment.

What the project does do is map the traffic-control problem into the kind of binary quadratic structure that quantum optimization methods are designed to handle. It then uses a low-energy search process whose design is inspired by the optimization philosophy of QAOA. This is why the label "quantum-inspired" is correct. It captures the fact that the model is guided by quantum optimization concepts without pretending that the final implementation already achieves hardware-quantum execution. In a research paper, this distinction is actually a strength, because it shows that the project is technically honest while still being conceptually ambitious.

Another reason this distinction matters is reproducibility. A fully quantum implementation would currently be difficult to reproduce across ordinary academic environments because access to suitable quantum hardware is limited, and hardware noise would complicate benchmarking. The quantum-inspired classical implementation used here is far easier to benchmark, inspect, and validate. It therefore provides a realistic and useful bridge between theoretical quantum optimization and present-day transportation simulation.

## 25. Novelty, Research Significance, and Contribution Positioning
The novelty of the project lies primarily in the integration of concepts rather than the invention of a single isolated algorithmic primitive. QUBO already exists, QAOA already exists, SUMO already exists, and graph-based prediction already exists. What is uncommon is the way these elements are combined in the current work: an image-derived traffic network is converted into a SUMO scenario, a graph-based predictor estimates congestion, a QUBO encodes signal decisions and coordination, a QAOA-style solver searches for low-energy assignments, and a hybrid controller refines the result through traffic-domain rules. This integrated architecture is the real contribution.

The research significance of the project comes from demonstrating that traffic signal control can be reframed as a mathematically explicit, computationally grounded optimization problem without losing interpretability. The project gives a concrete example of how Theory of Computation can guide the formulation of a real transportation problem, and how quantum-inspired methods can then be used to organize the search for good signal-control decisions. It also shows that explainability can coexist with optimization. Instead of producing only one scalar output, the project makes it possible to examine controller comparison tables, metric-specific graphs, time-series behavior, and spatial congestion maps. That makes the work stronger from both a systems perspective and a communication perspective.

## 26. Limitations and Current Boundaries
Although the project is strong as a research-oriented prototype, it still has important limitations that should be acknowledged clearly. First, the quantum component is quantum-inspired rather than fully hardware-quantum. Second, the image-derived scenario and compact network conversion are designed for benchmark clarity and presentation usefulness rather than fully lossless GIS fidelity. Third, the current benchmark focuses on a limited set of scenarios, and broader validation would strengthen the conclusions. Fourth, while the hybrid controller includes a policy-refinement component, the overall framework is still more optimization-driven than long-horizon reinforcement-learning-driven.

There are also engineering limitations. Traffic demand in the current implementation is simulated rather than imported from a live traffic authority feed. The benchmark is convincing for research demonstration, but it is not yet a field deployment. The project should therefore be presented as a carefully implemented and well-motivated prototype, not as a claim of immediate real-world deployment readiness. Making these limits explicit improves the credibility of the work and protects it from overstatement.

## 27. Future Scope
The future scope of the project is broad and meaningful. One strong direction is multi-scenario validation. The current image-derived interchange benchmark is useful, but future work should test the hybrid controller on arterial corridors, denser urban grids, and larger imported SUMO networks to better understand how the optimization scales. Another direction is deeper quantum integration. The present solver is QAOA-style and classical; a future version could connect the QUBO output to actual quantum software backends so that small benchmark instances can be compared against circuit-based or annealer-based workflows.

The graph-prediction layer can also be improved. At present, the predictor already performs adjacency-aware feature propagation, but future work could incorporate richer spatiotemporal graph neural methods, adaptive edge weighting, and stronger temporal memory. The explainability layer can be expanded too, for example by adding ablation studies, junction-level attribution reports, energy-component plots, and controller-confidence diagnostics. Another important direction is the integration of realistic OSM-based or measured traffic demand data, which would make the benchmark more representative of operational traffic conditions.

From a broader research standpoint, the project provides an excellent base for extending toward emergency-vehicle prioritization, multi-objective fairness-aware signal control, pedestrian-aware phasing, digital twins, and eventually hardware-assisted quantum optimization for reduced network instances. Therefore, the future scope is not minor polishing; it is the gradual movement from a strong research prototype toward a broader intelligent transportation optimization platform.

## 28. Conclusion
This project demonstrates that traffic signal coordination can be treated as a computationally structured optimization problem rather than a purely ad hoc control task. By representing the network as a graph, by mapping phase assignment into a QUBO objective, and by using a QAOA-style low-energy search together with hybrid traffic-domain rules, the system creates a meaningful bridge between Theory of Computation and intelligent traffic management. The resulting controller is not only optimization-driven but also explainable, benchmarkable, and practical within a SUMO simulation environment.

The work is especially valuable because it shows how abstract computational ideas become useful when they are connected to a concrete systems problem. Computational complexity explains why the traffic problem is difficult. Graph abstraction explains how junctions influence one another. Binary optimization explains how phase decisions can be encoded mathematically. Quantum-inspired search explains how low-energy assignments can be explored without exhaustive enumeration. Finally, the hybrid controller shows how all of these ideas can be brought back into the operational language of green phases, waiting time, queue control, throughput, and balanced corridor behavior.

From an academic standpoint, the project is strong because it is both conceptually rigorous and operationally grounded. It does not make exaggerated claims about full quantum deployment, yet it still uses the language and mathematics of quantum-inspired optimization in a meaningful and defensible way. Its benchmark outputs and explanatory analytics make it suitable for journal presentation, project defense, and future extension. In summary, the project succeeds in presenting a clear, research-worthy, and simulation-backed framework for explainable quantum-inspired traffic signal control.
